Varicose Vein Treatment Procedures

Explore modern and traditional treatments

1. Stripping

Stripping involves surgically removing the saphenous vein. Though effective, recurrence and complications such as DVT (5.3%), pulmonary embolism (0.06%), and infections (2.2%) can occur. The vein can regrow, and future use for bypass grafts is lost.

2. Other Surgical Options

  • CHIVA Method: Minimally invasive, preserves vein; more bruising than laser/RFA.
  • Vein Ligation: Ties off the vein without removing it; effective when perforators are competent.
  • Cryosurgery: A cryoprobe freezes the vein before removal; avoids distal incision.

3. Sclerotherapy

Injecting sclerosants (like STS, POL, or hypertonic saline) into veins causes them to shrink and fade. Foam variants allow larger areas to be treated. It’s effective for spider and residual varicose veins. Rare risks include clots, ulcers, and allergic reactions.

4. Endovenous Thermal Ablation

  • Laser (EVLT): More effective short-term than stripping. Risks: burns (0.4%), paresthesia (2.1%).
  • Radiofrequency (RFA): Fewer complications than surgery. Risks: DVT (0.57%), nerve damage (up to 15%).
  • Steam: Natural and aesthetic; minimal pain, fast recovery, and effective.

5. Medical Adhesive (Super Glue)

Using cyanoacrylate glue, this method closes off varicose veins via catheter and ultrasound guidance. Success rate is 96.8%, with no scarring or hospitalization.

6. Echotherapy (HIFU)

High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound (HIFU) treats veins from outside the body—no surgery, no scars, and immediate return to daily life.

7. Ambulatory Phlebectomy

Used in combination with thermal ablation. Surface veins are removed after proximal veins are ablated. Especially useful for branch varicosities.

Epidemiology

Varicose veins are common after age 50, especially in women. Risk factors include:

  • Family history
  • Smoking
  • Chronic constipation
  • Occupations involving long standing: waiters, nurses, conductors, actors, surgeons, security staff, etc.
